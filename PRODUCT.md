# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

**Primary: prospective undergraduate students.** Curious, exploring research opportunities, often new to machine learning and to academic research entirely. They're trying to answer "Is this lab somewhere I'd want to work? Can I see myself in this work?" They scan for tangible projects, real people, and whether they'd be welcomed.

**Secondary: the general public.** Non-specialists encountering the lab through press, search, or word-of-mouth. They want to understand what the lab actually does and why it matters, in language they can follow without an ML background.

**Tertiary: academic peers, collaborators, funders.** Skimming for credibility signals (publications, projects, people). Their needs are met by clear research lists and team pages; they are not the design's primary tilt.

## Product Purpose

The website is the public face of the Human-Machine Intelligent Systems Lab, a use-inspired research group applying machine learning to quality-of-life problems in medicine, biology, and teaching and learning.

Its job is to present the lab's work with high fidelity to undergraduates and a broad public audience. Not to build a brand for its own sake, but to make the research and its impact understandable, memorable, and inviting enough that prospective students reach out.

Success looks like: a curious sophomore lands on a research thread, follows the through-line from problem to method to real-world impact to the people behind it, and emails the PI.

## Brand Personality

**Three words: warm, human, approachable.**

- **Warm.** Not sterile, not corporate, not AI-startup. A reader should feel welcomed, not gated by jargon.
- **Human.** People, stories, and impact carry the site. The lab is led by humans working with humans on human problems.
- **Approachable.** An undergraduate with a vague interest in ML should feel like there is a way in. The site should never feel like a fortress of expertise.

Voice: plain, direct, generous. Explain the *why* before the *how*. Assume an interested undergraduate, not a peer reviewer.

## Anti-references

- **AI slop visual tropes.** Neon-on-black AI-demo pages, generic neural-net hero illustrations, gradient text, glassmorphism, dark-mode-with-purple-glow aesthetic. The lab works on ML; the site should not look like every ML company's landing page.
- **Card grids.** Uniform rows of equivalent icon+heading+text tiles. Particularly avoid cards as the primary layout pattern for projects, research, or team. The work is too varied for that flattening.
- **Default Bootstrap-academic look.** Three-column footer, stock-photo hero, tabbed bio. The kind of site any university IT department could spin up in an afternoon.
- **SaaS landing-page aesthetic.** "Get started" CTAs, gradient hero, feature grid with checkmarks, social-proof bar. This is a research lab, not a product launch.

## Design Principles

1. **The work is the design.** Research, projects, and the people doing them carry the visual interest. Chrome (typography, color, layout) supports them and gets out of the way. If a design choice draws attention to itself rather than to the work, it's wrong.

2. **Storytelling over cataloging.** Present research and projects as connected narratives, with image-and-text spreads that have rhythm, in the spirit of yalidymatos.com. Not grids of equivalent items. A reader should follow a through-line from problem to impact, not bounce across tiles.

3. **Warm, not sterile.** Restrained does not mean monochrome. Keep human warmth (a sympathetic palette, a sympathetic voice) so undergraduates and the general public feel invited in. Cold academic minimalism is its own anti-pattern.

4. **Plain language as a feature.** Every research description should explain the *why* and the *impact* in terms an interested non-specialist can follow. Jargon-first writing fails the primary audience even when it is accurate.

5. **Distinctive without chasing trends.** Avoid the visual reflexes that mark a site as "ML lab in 202X." The lab's longevity is the point; the design should age the same way.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

- **WCAG 2.1 AA compliance** as a baseline commitment. All interactive elements keyboard-navigable, color contrast meets AA on all text and meaningful UI, focus states visible.
- **Plain language** as an accessibility commitment as well as a strategic one. Undergraduates and general-public readers are part of the audience the site is *for*, not an afterthought.
- **Reduced-motion respect.** Any scroll-driven storytelling honors `prefers-reduced-motion` with a static fallback.
- **Image alt text** treated as content, not decoration. Every research and team photo carries a description that conveys the same information a sighted user gets.
